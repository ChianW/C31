# First Principle Thinking

> Trace back to the root, redefine — do not merely optimize.

## Core Definition

First Principles Thinking is a method of breaking down complex things to the most fundamental physical or axiomatic facts, and then rebuilding solutions from those "ground truths" — rather than relying on analogy or imitating convention.

## Analogical Thinking vs. First Principles

| Dimension | Analogical Thinking (conventional) | First Principles (ground-up innovation) |
|-----------|-------------------------------------|------------------------------------------|
| Starting point | Follow past experience, industry norms, or what others did | Strip away surface appearances and target the irreducible essential elements |
| Reasoning method | Inductive: others succeeded this way, so I'll do the same | Deductive: derive new solutions from fundamental laws and objective facts |
| End result | Incremental improvements and fine-tuning (optimizing what exists) | Innovative breakthroughs and reconstruction (redefining what exists) |

## Classic Case: Elon Musk and SpaceX

**Conventional thinking (analogical):**
> "Spacecraft have always been expensive — that's been true for decades. Buying a ready-made rocket is too costly, so lowering launch costs seems impossible."

**First Principles:**
> "What are the core materials in a rocket? Aluminum, titanium, copper, carbon fiber. These raw materials are actually quite cheap on commodity markets — roughly 2% of the rocket's total price. So instead of buying finished products, we buy the raw materials and build it ourselves from scratch."

**Result:** Launch costs dropped to one-tenth or even one-hundredth of the original.

## Practice Steps

When executing First Principles thinking, strictly follow these three steps:

### Step 1: Identify and Define the Problem
- Clearly state the specific problem to solve
- Example: "Why is the product too expensive?" "Why does no one use this feature?"

### Step 2: Decompose to Essential Facts
- Keep asking "why"
- Strip away all assumptions, conventions, and analogies
- Retain only undeniable physical laws, mathematical axioms, or core elements
- **Key question:** "If X were removed, would this thing still be itself?" If "no," X is essential. If "yes," X is an assumption.

### Step 3: Rebuild from Fundamentals
- Abandon "what does everyone else do"
- Starting from the ground-level facts found in Step 2, derive a completely new solution upward
- The answer may look entirely different from existing approaches

## Self-Check Questions (answer before each use)

1. Am I relying on analogy / experience / convention, or on ground-level facts?
2. If I remove the industry's default assumptions, what does the problem become?
3. From a physics / math / logic perspective, what is the minimum necessary composition of this thing?
4. Is my solution derived from "what everyone else does" or from "what this thing must be"?

## Common Pitfalls

- **Pseudo-first-principles:** Decomposing only to "I think" or "experience tells me," not to "physics/mathematics proves"
- **Over-decomposition:** Going all the way to quantum mechanics when it has no bearing on the actual problem. Decomposition depth should match problem complexity
- **Ignoring constraints:** Only decomposing without reconstructing — failing to build a viable solution from the ground up
